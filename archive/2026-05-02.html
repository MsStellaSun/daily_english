01
Short fragment
Flag any outstanding issues.
Directing attention to unresolved problems.
Examples
"At the end of the review, 'Flag any outstanding issues' was asked to ensure completeness."
"Before final approval, 'Flag any outstanding issues' is a reminder to check for errors."
Tone Tip
Quick way to identify and address unresolved matters.
02
Longer phrase
Let's review the budget breakdown.
Requesting a detailed examination of the financial allocation.
Examples
"During the meeting, 'Let's review the budget breakdown' was suggested to clarify departmental spending."
"Before approving the proposal, 'Let's review the budget breakdown' is essential for transparency."
Tone Tip
Use this to introduce a focused discussion on financial details.
03
Longer phrase
Let's discuss the client feedback.
Initiating a conversation about customer input.
Examples
"To improve service, 'Let's discuss the client feedback' was suggested in the team meeting."
"For product development, 'Let's discuss the client feedback' is a regular agenda item."
Tone Tip
Encourages customer-centric improvements.
04
Longer phrase
Could you share the market analysis?
Asking for a report or data on market trends.
Examples
"To prepare the strategy, 'Could you share the market analysis?' was asked to the research team."
"For the Q2 presentation, 'Could you share the market analysis?' is a necessary request."
Tone Tip
Polite and professional way to request data-driven insights.
05
Longer phrase
Let's align on the project timeline.
Seeking agreement on the schedule for a project.
Examples
"To ensure coordination, 'Let's align on the project timeline' was proposed by the project manager."
"For smooth execution, 'Let's align on the project timeline' is a critical step."
Tone Tip
Encourages teamwork and avoids miscommunication on deadlines.

About This Program

A daily habit for building professional English vocabulary and phrasing — designed for non-native speakers working in English-language environments.

Five carefully selected phrases are generated every day at midnight. Each one is practical, work-relevant, and explained with real examples and tone guidance.

Phrase Categories

  • Meeting language — start, navigate, and close meetings
  • Email & report writing — professional written tone
  • Polite disagreement — push back without friction
  • Presenting ideas — structure and deliver arguments
  • Asking for clarification — professionally and confidently
  • Transitions & wrap-up — connect and conclude

How to Use It

Read each phrase in the morning or evening. Try to use at least one in your actual work that day — in a meeting, an email, or a report.

The best learning happens when you connect a phrase to a real situation you experienced.
