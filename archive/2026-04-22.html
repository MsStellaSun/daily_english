01
Polite Disagreement
I hear you, but there is a risk that...
Use this to acknowledge the other person's perspective without agreeing with it, specifically when introducing a potential downside or challenge.
Examples
"I hear you, but there is a risk that this timeline won't accommodate the client's feedback without rushing the final deliverables."
"I hear you, but there is a risk that expanding the team too quickly will impact our current quality standards."
Tone Tip
This keeps you collaborative rather than confrontational by framing your objection around a logical risk rather than a personal opinion.
02
Meetings
To pick up on a point raised earlier...
A professional way to reintroduce a topic or specific idea mentioned previously in the conversation to ensure continuity and build on the discussion.
Examples
"To pick up on a point raised earlier regarding the Q3 budget, we might need to allocate more funds to marketing."
"To pick up on a point raised earlier about the server migration, I've prepared a timeline for the QA team to review."
Tone Tip
Use this instead of simple transitions like 'Also' or 'Too' to show you are listening actively and organizing the information logically.
03
Presenting
This framework allows us to...
Used to introduce a specific structure, model, or method to the audience, immediately explaining its function or the specific benefit it provides.
Examples
"This framework allows us to visualize the data trends more clearly than a standard spreadsheet would."
"This framework allows us to isolate the key variables without getting lost in the operational details."
Tone Tip
Follow this phrase immediately with the result or action; otherwise, you may confuse the listener with abstract terminology.
04
Transitions
Does that clarify the situation?
A polite closing question used at the end of a conversation or meeting to ensure the listener understands the action plan before you leave the topic.
Examples
"We have agreed on the new launch date, and Sarah will handle the press release. Does that clarify the situation?"
"I've outlined the troubleshooting steps, and you should see a resolution by tomorrow morning. Does that clarify the situation?"
Tone Tip
Do not use this if you are actually confused about the information; save it for when you are explaining something to confirm understanding.
05
Reports & Writing
This is designed to...
A strong way to explain the purpose or intention behind a document, report, or update in a professional context.
Examples
"The new dashboard is designed to track real-time inventory levels more accurately for the procurement team."
"This policy update is designed to streamline the approval process for remote reimbursements and reduce administrative overhead."
Tone Tip
Use 'designed to' to sound purposeful and strategic; avoid using the weaker 'is to' as it can sound passive or grammatically incomplete.

About This Program

A daily habit for building professional English vocabulary and phrasing — designed for non-native speakers working in English-language environments.

Five carefully selected phrases are generated every day at midnight. Each one is practical, work-relevant, and explained with real examples and tone guidance.

Phrase Categories

  • Meeting language — start, navigate, and close meetings
  • Email & report writing — professional written tone
  • Polite disagreement — push back without friction
  • Presenting ideas — structure and deliver arguments
  • Asking for clarification — professionally and confidently
  • Transitions & wrap-up — connect and conclude

How to Use It

Read each phrase in the morning or evening. Try to use at least one in your actual work that day — in a meeting, an email, or a report.

The best learning happens when you connect a phrase to a real situation you experienced.
