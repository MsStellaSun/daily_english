01
Polite Disagreement
I see where you're coming from, but...
This phrase acknowledges the other person's perspective before presenting a counterpoint, which softens the tone of a disagreement.
Examples
"I see where you're coming from, but we don't have the budget for that right now."
"I see where you're coming from, but I think the timeline is too tight for this approach."
Tone Tip
Avoid using this if you don't actually understand their point, as it can come across as insincere.
02
Reports & Writing
I wanted to flag this for your attention.
Used to highlight a critical issue, a deadline, or a document that requires urgent review or approval.
Examples
"I wanted to flag this for your attention regarding the compliance deadline."
"I wanted to flag this for your attention to ensure the contract terms are correct."
Tone Tip
Use this specifically for urgent matters rather than routine updates.
03
Clarification
Could you clarify what you mean by...
A professional way to ask for more details on a specific term or concept to ensure you are on the same page.
Examples
"Could you clarify what you mean by scalability in this context?"
"Could you clarify what you mean by the target audience for this campaign?"
Tone Tip
Asking this early prevents you from making assumptions that could lead to costly errors.
04
Transitions
Let's circle back to this later.
A polite way to defer a topic to a future meeting or time, keeping the current discussion focused.
Examples
"Let's circle back to this later when we have more data."
"That's a great point, let's circle back to this during our next sync."
Tone Tip
Ensure you actually intend to discuss the topic later; otherwise, it can frustrate the listener.
05
Meetings
I'd like to pick up on a point you made earlier.
A structured transition used to refer back to something previously discussed to maintain continuity.
Examples
"I'd like to pick up on a point you made earlier regarding the budget."
"I'd like to pick up on a point you made earlier about the new software."
Tone Tip
Use this to build a logical flow rather than interrupting the speaker abruptly.

About This Program

A daily habit for building professional English vocabulary and phrasing — designed for non-native speakers working in English-language environments.

Five carefully selected phrases are generated every day at midnight. Each one is practical, work-relevant, and explained with real examples and tone guidance.

Phrase Categories

  • Meeting language — start, navigate, and close meetings
  • Email & report writing — professional written tone
  • Polite disagreement — push back without friction
  • Presenting ideas — structure and deliver arguments
  • Asking for clarification — professionally and confidently
  • Transitions & wrap-up — connect and conclude

How to Use It

Read each phrase in the morning or evening. Try to use at least one in your actual work that day — in a meeting, an email, or a report.

The best learning happens when you connect a phrase to a real situation you experienced.
