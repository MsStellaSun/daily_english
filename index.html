5 Professional Phrases

Read, understand, and try using one in your work today.

01
Meetings
To level-set
To make sure everyone in a meeting or discussion has the same understanding before moving forward. Use it at the start of a meeting or before a key decision.
Examples
"Before we dive in, let me level-set on where we are with the project timeline."
"I just want to level-set on our goals for this quarter so we're all aligned."
Tone Tip
Register: Common in corporate meetings, especially in tech and consulting. Avoid in formal written reports — prefer "to establish a shared understanding" there.
02
Reports & Writing
As outlined in …
Used to refer back to something already written or presented. It avoids repetition and signals that the information has already been established.
Examples
"As outlined in the previous section, the budget will need to be revised."
"As outlined in my email last Friday, the deadline has moved to May 3rd."
Tone Tip
Common mistake: Don't say "as I outlined before" in written reports — it sounds too informal. Use "as outlined in [section/document]" for a polished, objective tone.
03
Meetings
I'd like to flag …
A professional way to bring attention to a concern, risk, or issue without sounding alarming. It shows you are being proactive and responsible.
Examples
"I'd like to flag a potential conflict with our current vendor contract."
"Before we finalize this, I'd like to flag that we haven't confirmed the budget approval yet."
Tone Tip
Why it works: "Flag" feels neutral and collaborative, not accusatory. Much softer than "there's a problem with…" — it invites discussion rather than creating tension.
04
Polite Disagreement
I see where you're coming from, and …
A way to acknowledge someone's point before offering a different view. It shows respect for their perspective while opening space for your own.
Examples
"I see where you're coming from, and I do think we need to consider the cost implications first."
"I see where you're coming from, and it might be worth looking at the data before we decide."
Tone Tip
Key detail: Use "and" — not "but" — after this phrase. "But" can still sound dismissive. "And" keeps the tone genuinely collaborative.
05
Transitions
To that end, …
Means "in order to achieve that goal." Use it to connect a stated objective to the action or recommendation that follows. Common in reports and presentations.
Examples
"Our goal is to reduce onboarding time by 30%. To that end, we recommend simplifying the training materials."
"We need stronger cross-team collaboration. To that end, I'm proposing a weekly sync between departments."
Tone Tip
Register: Slightly formal — great for written reports and presentations. In casual meetings, "so" or "with that in mind" sounds more natural.

About This Program

A daily habit for building professional English vocabulary and phrasing — designed for non-native speakers working in English-language environments.

Five carefully selected phrases land every evening. Each one is practical, work-relevant, and explained with real examples and tone guidance.

Phrase Categories

  • Meeting language — start, navigate, and close meetings
  • Email & report writing — professional written tone
  • Polite disagreement — push back without friction
  • Presenting ideas — structure and deliver arguments
  • Asking for clarification — professionally and confidently
  • Transitions & wrap-up — connect and conclude

How to Use It

Read each phrase in the morning or evening. Try to use at least one in your actual work that day — in a meeting, an email, or a report.

The best learning happens when you connect a phrase to a real situation you experienced.
